The honorable Charles Smith, Miss Sarah's brother, was walking swiftly uptown from Mr. Easterly's Wall Street office and his face was pale. At last, the cotton combine was to all appearances in assured fact, and he was slated for the Senate. Why should he not be his other men? She was not herself a notably intelligent woman. She greatly admired intelligence or whatever looked to her like intelligence and others. She awaited her guess she surveyed the table with both satisfaction and as quietude, for her social functions were few. Tonight there were, she checked them off on her fingers, served James Criton, the rich English manufacturer and Lady Criton. Mr. and Mrs. Vanderpool, Mr. Harry Cresswell and his sister, John Taylor and his sister and Mr. Charles Smith, whom the evening papers mentioned as likely to be Udynastate Senator from New Jersey, a selection of guests that had been determined unknown to the hostess by the meeting of Cotton Empress earlier in the day. Mrs. Gray had met Southerners before, but not intimately, and she always had in mind vividly their cruelty to poor Negroes, a subject she made a point of introducing forthwith. She was therefore most agreeably surprised to hear Mr. Cresswell express himself so cordially as approving of Negro education. Do you believe in some education? Ask Mary Taylor. I believe in the train in of people to the Hask capacity. The Englishman here heartily seconded him.
