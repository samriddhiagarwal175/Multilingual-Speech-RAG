At last he came out of them and wiped his face vigorously. On the 6th of April, 1830, the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints was formerly organized and thus took on a legal existence. Its origin was small, a germ, an insignificant seed, hardly to be thought of as likely to arouse opposition. Instead of but six regularly affiliated members, and at most two square of adherence, the organization numbers today many hundred thousand souls. In place of a single hamlet, in the smallest corner of which the members could have congregated. There now are about seventy stakes of Zion and about seven hundred organized words, each ward and stake with its full complement of officers and pre-stood organizations. The practice of gathering its proselites into one place prevents the building up and strengthening of foreign branches. And in as much as extensive and strong organizations are seldom met with a broad, very erroneous ideas exist, considering the strength of the church. Nevertheless, the mustard seed, among the smallest of all seeds, has attained the proportions of a tree, and the birds of the air are nesting in its branches. The acorn is now in oak, offering protection and the suites of satisfaction, to every earnest pilgrim, journeying its way for truth. Their eyes were from the first turned in anticipation toward the evening sun. Not merely that the work of cross-lighting should be carried on in the west, but that the headquarters of the church should be there established. The Book of Mormon had taught the people the true origin and destiny of the American Indians, and toward this dark skinned remnant of a once-mighty people. The missionaries of Mormonism early turned their eyes, and with their eyes went their hearts and their hopes. It is notable that the Indian tribes have generally regarded their religion of the latter day Saints with favor, seeing in the Book of Mormon striking agreement with their own traditions.
